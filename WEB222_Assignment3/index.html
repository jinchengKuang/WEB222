Introduction

The groundhog, is also called woodchuck, whose bionomial name is Marmota Monax, belongs to class Mammalia, order Rodentia, and family Sciuridae. The groundhog lives in North America, including east of the US, Canada, and cross into Alaska. Unlike other marmots mostly living in rocky and mountainous areas, the groundhog lives at lowlands.

Size

Groundhog is one of the largest sciurid, weighing from 4.4 to 13.9 pounds. Male groundhog is generally larger than females. Groundhogs will be heavier during autumn because they will be hibernating. Length from 16.5 to 27.0 inches including tail, which is about one-fourth of the body length, from 3.7 to 7.4 inches.

Survaval

On average, groundhogs can live two to three years, with up to six year when they are living in the wild. If they are well taken care of, they can live up to 14 years. Groundhogs like living in human-developed areas because there are second-grow trees and less predators.

In the wild, groundhogs have several predators, including stealth stalkers who can follow and catch them, larger diggers who can dig them from their place, and overpower hunters like coyotes. Younger groundhogs may be catched by many other predators. To escape from their predators, groundhogs can climb trees and retreat back to their burrows, ready to fight off.

Habits

Groundhogs primarily have grass and vegetations as their food, such as dandelion and coltsfoot. They spend their time in summers and falls eating about one pound of food a day within 50 to 150 feet from their burrows, eating enough in warmer seasons helps stuff themselves for winter when they are not eating. Groundhogs hibernate in their winter burrows during winter. While hibernating, their heartbeats drop to 5 beats per second, 2 breaths per minutes, and body temperature drops to 2.77 Celsius degrees.

Reproduction

Groundhogs' breeding seasons will be early March to late April after their hibernation. Gestation period will be around 32 days, during this period, male groundhog will remain with female groundhog, but may leave after young groundhog approaches. They have only one litter per year, usually two to six young per litter. When young's fur is grown and when they can see, groundhog mothers will lead them to the wild. Young groundhogs will leave their mothers for their own homes when they become mature.

Relationship with Human

Because groundhogs eat vegetables, and they dig burrows which are undermining the foundations, they bring nuisance to farms and gardens.

Meanwhile, the burrows of groundhogs provide homes for other animals, most of them eat creatures who are destroying the farm. Besides, as groundhogs digging their burrows, they can shift up to 700 pounds of soil out, which helps farms improve the quality of soil.

History of Groundhog Day comes from a believe that a groundhog comes out of the burrow, and on this day, if it sees its shadow, it will be back to its burrow, and winter will be six weeks more; otherwise, spring will arrive earlier. Although studies found that nothing relates groundhogs see their shadow and the arrival time of spring, Groundhog Day is still a North American tradition.

Groundhogs also help medical research on HBV because it is similar to WHV. Medical researches on groundhogs may lead to many benefits for human.
